# Provenance Guard — Planning Document

## Architecture

### System Narrative
A creator submits text to `POST /submit`. The rate limiter checks their quota first — if exceeded, 
the request is rejected with a 429. If not, the text enters the detection pipeline where two 
independent signals run: an LLM call to Groq (semantic/stylistic analysis) and a set of 
stylometric heuristics computed locally (sentence length variance, type-token ratio, punctuation 
density). Each signal returns a score between 0.0–1.0. The confidence scorer blends those scores 
into a final confidence value using a weighted formula. That value maps to one of four transparency 
label variants which is returned to the caller alongside the result and confidence score. Every 
decision is written to the audit log.

For appeals, a creator submits to `POST /appeal` with their submission ID and written reasoning. 
The system updates the content status to "under_review", logs the appeal alongside the original 
decision, and returns a confirmation.

## Detection Signals

### Signal 1 — LLM Semantic Analysis (Groq)
**What it measures:** The semantic and stylistic coherence of the text as a whole. Groq reads the 
text holistically and assesses whether it exhibits patterns common to AI generation: unnatural 
smoothness, overly balanced paragraph structure, generic confidence, and impersonal vocabulary 
choices that are technically correct but lack human idiosyncrasy.

**Why it gets higher weight:** It captures the feel of the writing the same way a human reader 
would notice something feels "off." This is a richer signal than structural statistics alone.

**Output:** A score between 0.0–1.0 where 1.0 = high confidence AI-generated.

**Blind spot:** Short texts give the model very little to work with. A skilled writer deliberately 
mimicking AI style could also fool it.

---

### Signal 2 — Stylometric Heuristics (pure Python)
**What it measures:** Three structural statistical properties of the text:
- **Sentence length variance** — humans write sentences of wildly different lengths; AI tends 
  toward uniformity
- **Type-token ratio (TTR)** — unique words divided by total words; AI reuses safe vocabulary 
  more, producing a lower TTR
- **Punctuation density** — humans use dashes, ellipses, and irregular punctuation; AI defaults 
  to clean periods and commas

Each heuristic is normalized to 0.0–1.0 and averaged into a single signal score.

**Output:** A score between 0.0–1.0 where 1.0 = high confidence AI-generated.

**Blind spot:** Cannot detect meaning — only shape. A human writing formal or legal text would 
score AI-like on all three metrics due to the nature of that writing style.

---

### Confidence Scoring Formula
confidence = (signal1 * 0.65) + (signal2 * 0.35)
Signal 1 receives the higher weight because it captures semantic meaning holistically. Signal 2 
is a supporting structural signal. When the two signals agree, the confidence score is pushed 
toward the extremes. When they disagree, the weighted blend pulls toward the middle — landing 
in the uncertain range, which is the correct behavior when evidence is mixed.

## Uncertainty Representation

### Confidence Thresholds
| Range | Label |
|-------|-------|
| 0.0 – 0.40 | Likely human |
| 0.41 – 0.52 | Uncertain, leaning human |
| 0.53 – 0.64 | Uncertain, leaning AI |
| 0.65 – 1.0 | Likely AI |

The uncertain band is intentionally wide. On a creative platform, a false positive (labeling a 
human's work as AI) is more harmful than a false negative. When evidence is mixed, the system 
defaults toward caution rather than accusation. The likely human threshold is set at 0.40 rather 
than 0.50 to further reflect this asymmetry.

---

## Transparency Labels

Four label variants based on confidence range:

**Likely human** (≤ 0.40):
> "Our system found no significant indicators of AI generation in this content."

**Uncertain, leaning human** (0.41 – 0.52):
> "Our system could not confidently determine whether this content was written by a human or 
> generated by AI, however it is leaning toward human-written."

**Uncertain, leaning AI** (0.53 – 0.64):
> "Our system could not confidently determine whether this content was written by a human or 
> generated by AI, however it is leaning toward AI-generated."

**Likely AI** (≥ 0.65):
> "Our system found strong indicators that this content may have been AI-generated. The author 
> can contest this below."

## Appeals Workflow

**Who can appeal:** Only the creator who submitted the content, matched by `creator_id`.

**What they provide:**
- `submission_id` — identifies which decision is being contested
- `reasoning` — written explanation of why they believe the classification is wrong

**What the system does:**
1. Validates that the `creator_id` matches the original submission
2. Updates the content status from `"complete"` to `"under_review"`
3. Logs the appeal alongside the original decision in the audit log
4. Returns a 202 confirmation to the creator

**What a human reviewer sees in the appeal queue:**
- The original submitted text
- The confidence score at time of decision
- Which signals fired and their individual scores
- The transparency label that was shown to the user
- The creator's written reasoning

## Anticipated Edge Cases

**1. Poetry with repetitive structure**
A poem using short lines, repeated phrases, and minimal punctuation variety will score AI-like 
on stylometrics due to low sentence length variance and low TTR — even if entirely human-written. 
The poetic form itself mimics the structural patterns the heuristics flag.

**2. Non-native English speaker writing**
Careful, conservative sentence construction and limited vocabulary range (avoiding words the 
writer is unsure of) produces low TTR and uniform sentence structure — both AI-like signals — 
even though the writing is entirely human. Both signals could fire incorrectly here.

should land ≥ 0.65, a clearly human text should land ≤ 0.40.
